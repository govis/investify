### Investment Thesis

Geopolitical risk is no longer a peripheral tail-risk event; it is the dominant macroeconomic factor shaping global capital flows and supply chain architecture. The return of President Donald Trump to the White House in 2025 amplified protectionist trade policies and introduced significant unpredictability into U.S. foreign alliances, hastening the breakdown of multilateral norms.22 The global security environment deteriorated sharply in 2026, highlighted by the outbreak of a direct, state-on-state war between Israel and Iran, which featured direct military intervention by the United States.22

This conflict, alongside the grinding war of attrition in Ukraine, has demonstrated the critical necessity of advanced, high-technology defense systems and the rapid depletion of existing munitions stockpiles. Unlike symmetric wars of the 20th century, modern conflicts are defined by "360-degree" threats involving sophisticated drone swarms, hypersonic glide vehicles, and automated loitering munitions.23 Consequently, global defense procurement is shifting heavily toward advanced air-defense systems, directed-energy weapons (lasers), and AI-enabled fire control systems.26 The world's largest defense contractors are currently operating with historic, multi-year order backlogs. Following a White House mandate to "quadruple production" of exquisite-class weaponry, U.S. defense primes are expanding capacity rapidly.28 The U.S. defense budget, already the largest globally at nearly $1 trillion in 2025, is on a trajectory to reach $1.5 trillion by 2027 under current administration plans.28

Israeli defense firms are acting as the technological vanguard in this new paradigm. Benefiting from real-world battlefield testing of their systems, these companies have seen exponential revenue growth and international demand. Elbit Systems, for instance, ended 2025 with a record $28.1 billion backlog, driven by European and North American procurement of its autonomous systems and electronic warfare suites.29 Smaller, specialized entities are experiencing even more dramatic growth; companies like NextVision, producing ultra-lightweight stabilized cameras for tactical drones, and Smart Shooter, developing AI fire control systems that transform standard rifles into counter-drone platforms, have become critical assets for Western militaries seeking to combat asymmetric threats.27 Furthermore, the deployment of interceptors like Lockheed Martin's THAAD and RTX's Patriot systems during the Iran conflict marked the U.S. military's single-largest use of advanced missile defenses in history, guaranteeing massive replenishment contracts.30

Simultaneously, the theater of conflict extends deeply into global energy markets and critical infrastructure. The January 2026 U.S. military ouster and arrest of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro represents a drastic, paradigm-shifting intervention aimed at restructuring global oil flows during a period of acute Middle Eastern instability.31 Following the regime change, the U.S. Treasury issued broad authorizations allowing the state-owned oil company, Petróleos de Venezuela S.A. (PDVSA), to sell heavy crude directly to U.S. companies.31 However, this is not an immediate panacea for global oil supplies. Decades of corruption, mismanagement, and underinvestment mean that restoring Venezuelan production from its current state of approximately 400,000 barrels per day to historic highs of over 2 million barrels per day will require 12 to 18 months of intensive, multi-billion-dollar capital investment.31 Western energy majors like Chevron and ExxonMobil are positioned to lead this revitalization, operating through strict U.S.-controlled financial clearinghouses designed to prevent capital flight and exclude adversaries like Russia and China from the recovery process.31

The vulnerability of extended maritime chokepoints and the persistent threat of broad tariffs have accelerated corporate "reshoring" and "nearshoring" initiatives across all industrial sectors. Roughly 29% of surveyed U.S. manufacturers report having completed reshoring operations, while another 45% are actively pursuing relocation plans to ensure operational agility and supplier diversification.25 Industrial policies and federal grants are catalyzing this transition, funneling billions into the domestic manufacturing of semiconductors, high-voltage transformers, and battery components to insulate the defense and technological bases from foreign coercion.36 This marks a definitive, irreversible shift in corporate inventory strategy from optimized, highly efficient "just-in-time" models to robust, capital-intensive "just-in-case" architectures.3
