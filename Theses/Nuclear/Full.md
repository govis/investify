### Investment Thesis

The global pursuit of clean energy has collided with the physical realities of grid stability, resulting in a profound shift in energy policy. Wind and solar power, while essential components of the transition (projected to account for 19% of global electricity generation by 2026), suffer from inherent intermittency.2 They cannot independently support the rigid demands of modern digital infrastructure without cost-prohibitive, grid-scale battery storage.21 AI compute demand has emerged as the strongest commercial driver for nuclear energy, fundamentally reshaping grid planning priorities.20 Unlike historical residential or commercial consumption patterns, AI workloads require continuous, high-density baseload power. A single AI training cluster can demand up to 100 megawatts (MW)—equivalent to the power draw of 80,000 homes—making nuclear power a non-negotiable infrastructure requirement for technology companies prioritizing both reliability and climate commitments.20 According to the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), global nuclear power capacity could double by 2050, reaching nearly 1,000 GW(e) under high-case scenarios.20

This expansion of nuclear capacity is unfolding through a pragmatic, multi-phased strategy designed to mitigate the historical risks of cost overruns and construction delays.21 Phase 1 (2022–2026) involves executing operating life extensions for existing reactor fleets.21 With a median global fleet age of 32 years, securing license extensions provides the most immediate and cost-effective method to preserve crucial baseload capacity until new construction comes online.21 Phase 2 (2024–2028) centers on the reactivation of suspended facilities, prominently highlighted by the unprecedented restart of the Three Mile Island facility in the United States, driven directly by hyperscaler power purchase agreements.21 Phase 3 (2026–2035) will focus on brownfield development, whereby utilities leverage existing or former nuclear sites for new reactor construction, significantly reducing development risks by utilizing established infrastructure, existing grid interconnections, and established community acceptance.21

Phase 4 (2030–2040) represents the mass commercialization and deployment of Small Modular Reactors (SMRs).21 The SMR landscape is highly dynamic and fiercely competitive, with the OECD Nuclear Energy Agency identifying 127 distinct technologies and 74 actively advancing designs globally.19 SMRs address the fundamental vulnerabilities of the traditional nuclear sector by utilizing standardized, factory-produced modular units that are assembled on-site, vastly improving quality control and reducing financing costs.21 Furthermore, their smaller geographic footprints (typically 20–300 MW) allow for unprecedented flexibility; they can be co-located directly with industrial applications, off-grid mining operations, or dedicated hyperscale data centers.19 Regulatory modernization is accelerating this timeline. In the United States, the development of the "Part 53" framework under the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, mandated by the Nuclear Energy Innovation and Modernization Act, is establishing efficient licensing pathways specifically tailored for these advanced designs.21

Despite immense policy and commercial momentum, the sector's growth is heavily constrained by an atrophied fuel supply chain. Following decades of underinvestment and depressed commodity pricing, a projected structural supply deficit in mined uranium is mathematically guaranteed by the 2030s as incremental demand from plant restarts and new construction outpaces primary production from existing and planned mines.21 Compounding this dynamic is a severe, geopolitically sensitive bottleneck in uranium conversion and enrichment capacity, which remains uncomfortably concentrated in Russia and China.21 Advanced SMR designs face a particularly acute vulnerability: their reliance on High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (HALEU), which is enriched to 5–20% U-235.21 Global supply of HALEU is currently highly limited and primarily controlled by Russian state entities.21 The strategic necessity of reshoring the Western nuclear fuel cycle is driving urgent, capital-intensive investments backed by sovereign incentives, creating a highly favorable pricing environment for Western uranium miners and enrichment specialists.21
