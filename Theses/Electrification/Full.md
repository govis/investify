### Investment Thesis

The fundamental quandary defining the industrial landscape of the late 2020s is that while macroeconomic mandates and technological evolution dictate rapid electrification, the physical supply of necessary raw materials cannot be scaled with equal velocity. Global electricity demand is undergoing a paradigm shift. Forecasts indicate an increase of 3.3% in 2025 and 3.7% in 2026, marking some of the highest sustained growth rates observed over the last decade.1 This growth is structurally resilient; despite the IMF's downgrade of global GDP growth amid trade tariff uncertainties, the sheer volume of power required by air conditioning, EVs, heat pumps, and data centers will push global electricity consumption to a new high of over 29,000 TWh by 2026\.1 Electricity demand is now set to rise more than twice as fast as total energy demand.1

Copper is at the absolute epicenter of this electrification crisis. A comprehensive analysis by S\&P Global projects that the accelerating pace of electrification will swell global copper demand by 50%, reaching 42 million metric tons annually by 2040\.8 This demand is highly inelastic, driven by vectors that must operate concurrently and cannot easily substitute the metal due to its superior electrical conductivity. National security and defense procurement, AI computational infrastructure, the modernization of aging electrical grids to accommodate decentralized renewable generation, and the mass electrification of global transport are all scaling simultaneously.8

Conversely, the upstream mining sector is fundamentally ill-equipped to meet this demand surge. Decades of depressed commodity prices prior to 2020 led to chronic underinvestment in geological exploration and capital expenditure. Developing a new Tier-1 copper mine frequently takes between 15 and 20 years from discovery to commercial production. As a result, global primary copper production is projected to peak at 33 million metric tons in 2030 and decline thereafter.8 This decline is exacerbated by degrading ore grades in legacy mines, acute water scarcity in primary producing regions like Chile and Peru, and escalating geopolitical risks that deter long-term capital deployment.8 Even under highly optimistic scenarios where global copper recycling output more than doubles—from 4 million metric tons today to 10 million metric tons by 2040—the market will still face a staggering 25% shortfall relative to demand.8 This 10 million metric ton gap constitutes a systemic bottleneck to technological innovation and virtually guarantees elevated, structurally higher copper prices throughout the 2030s to incentivize marginal supply.8

Parallel to the base metals crisis is a profound, evolving dynamic within Platinum Group Metals (PGMs). In 2025, platinum experienced a massive 127% price rally, driven by a potent combination of macropolitical shocks, depleted above-ground stocks, and severe physical market tightness.9 The market dynamics were fundamentally altered when leasing costs rose to unsustainably high levels by mid-2025\.9 This forced industrial end-users, who traditionally leased metal for catalytic and chemical processes, to shift toward outright ownership.9 This pivot drained massive volumes of metal from the lease market, effectively reducing liquidity, sustaining higher lease rates, and perpetuating a feedback loop of physical tightness into 2026\.9 The market is now forecast to record its third consecutive significant annual deficit, driving above-ground stocks down to a mere five months of demand cover.13

While bearish sentiment previously suppressed PGM valuations due to the anticipated decline of internal combustion engine (ICE) catalytic converters in the face of EV adoption, this narrative has been forcefully upended by the rapid advancement of the hydrogen economy.9 By 2030, hydrogen end-uses—particularly Heavy-Duty Fuel Cell Electric Vehicles (FCEVs), which require substantial platinum loadings per vehicle—are projected to account for 11% of total annual platinum demand, representing approximately 875 koz.9 As FCEVs approach cost parity with traditional diesel vehicles in key markets like Europe and China, platinum demand growth from the hydrogen sector is expected to fully offset the declining autocatalyst demand, permanently entrenching market deficits.9 Furthermore, the development of Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF), which relies heavily on platinum catalysts during the refining process, presents a massive secondary demand shock. If SAF scales to 25% of global jet fuel by 2050, it could increase annual platinum demand from the aviation sector ninefold to over 260 koz.9 Investors must also note the geographical concentration of supply; China's dominance in midstream capacity for critical metals (aluminum, graphite, rare earths) and its recent removal of VAT exemptions on platinum imports highlight the vulnerability of Western supply chains and underscore the strategic imperative of domestic mineral security.7
